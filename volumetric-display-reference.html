02

IR Feedback Loop — How It Works

The IR circuit is the angular position encoder — the heartbeat of the entire display. Without knowing the rotor's angular position, the MCU cannot know which column of the cylindrical display it is currently painting, and the image smears into an unreadable blur.

Physical Setup

A TCRT5000 reflective IR sensor mounts stationary on the base, pointing upward at the underside of the spinning rotor PCB. A small strip of white soldermask (or reflective tape) on the PCB underside acts as the reflector — it passes over the sensor once per revolution, producing a sharp pulse.

One tab per revolution = one sync pulse per revolution. That is all that is needed.

Signal Processing

The TCRT5000 output connects to a GPIO interrupt pin on the base MCU (STM32G031). On each rising edge the MCU records the timestamp using a hardware timer. The period between two successive pulses gives the exact revolution time.

RPM = 60 / period_in_seconds. The PID loop uses this computed RPM against the 6000 RPM setpoint to adjust PWM duty cycle on the motor MOSFET.

Column Timing on the Rotor

At 6000 RPM, one full revolution takes exactly 10ms. With 12 angular columns, each column's time window is 10ms ÷ 12 = 0.833ms. The ESP32-S3 uses a hardware timer (not a software loop) to fire an ISR every 0.833ms, advancing the column index from 0 to 11. On each timer tick, the current column's pixel data is burst-sent over SPI to the APA102C chain. The sync pulse resets the column counter to 0 at the start of each revolution.

Schmitt trigger note: Enable the Schmitt trigger on the ESP32-S3 GPIO input that receives the IR signal. Both the ESP32-S3 and STM32 have configurable Schmitt triggers on their GPIO. This turns the analog IR detector output into a clean digital edge with hysteresis, eliminating bounce and false triggers near the threshold. Without it, a noisy IR signal causes double-triggers and image jitter.

Speed Control PID

The STM32G031 runs a simple PID loop in its IR interrupt handler. Start with P-only during bring-up, then add I-term once the motor's response is characterized.

// simplified PID in interrupt context float rpm = 60.0f / period_s; float error = 6000.0f - rpm; integral += error * dt; float duty = Kp*error + Ki*integral; duty = clamp(duty, 0.0f, 1.0f); TIM1->CCR1 = (uint16_t)(duty * PWM_PERIOD);

LED Selection — APA102C (2020)

Why APA102C

Two-wire SPI protocol (CLK + DATA). No timing constraints unlike WS2812B. Can run at up to 20 MHz SPI clock — massively faster than needed, which gives margin for safe operation at 6000 RPM column timing.

Why 2020 package

2mm × 2mm footprint. Fits 12 LEDs across a 15mm PCB width with comfortable spacing. Lower mass than 3535 or 5050, reducing centrifugal stress on solder joints at high RPM.

SK9822 Alternative

Drop-in APA102C clone with a faster internal PWM clock (up to 4.7kHz vs APA102's ~440Hz). Slightly less flicker at low brightness. Available on LCSC as a more consistent supply source.

SPI decoupling: Place 100nF ceramic decoupling capacitors on the 5V supply rail at each group of 4–6 APA102C LEDs on the rotor PCB. At high centrifugal acceleration, supply inductance is effectively increased — decoupling capacitors prevent supply rail droop during simultaneous LED updates.

Why This Form Factor

The LP803450 is 8mm thick — this is the critical dimension. The base interior has roughly 25–30mm of height after the motor, PCB, and coil are accounted for. A flat LiPo lying horizontal consumes only 8mm of that. An 18650 cylindrical cell would need to stand upright (65mm tall, exceeds budget) or lie on its side (18mm diameter, fights the motor pocket).

Going above 1500mAh pushes you into physically larger cells (LP804050 = 8×40×50mm, still manageable, ~2000mAh) without meaningful design changes. Going below 1000mAh results in under 1 hour runtime at full brightness — uncomfortable for a display you want to run passively.

PCM / Protection Note

Do not double-protect. The BQ25895 handles all overvoltage, undervoltage, and overcurrent protection at the system level. Source a bare pouch cell without built-in PCM if possible. If the cell comes with a PCM (as Adafruit's do), it is still fine — just note the PCM's 2.75V cutoff may trigger before the BQ25895 threshold under some edge cases.

Inductive Power Transfer

Why Inductive (vs Slip Rings)

Slip rings provide reliable electrical contact through rotating interfaces, but they add mechanical friction, wear over time, generate sparks under load, and require precise alignment. At 6000 RPM, even a small amount of wear debris can cause signal noise in the IR feedback circuit.

Inductive transfer using a Qi-compatible 5W coil set is frictionless, wear-free, and rotationally symmetric — coupling is constant throughout the revolution since both coils share the same rotational axis.

Coil Configuration

StandardQi 5W compatible
TX coil locationBase top face, flush-mounted
RX coil locationRotor shaft base, bottom of PCB
Gap1–3 mm coaxial
Efficiency~70–75%
TX voltage5V (from TPS61023 boost)
RX output5V DC after rectifier
Max power5W (~1A at 5V)
Gap is critical. Qi coil efficiency drops sharply beyond 3mm gap. Design the motor shaft adapter such that the RX coil sits 1–2mm above the base top surface when the rotor is seated. Prototype the coil alignment before committing to the final enclosure geometry.

Important Limitation

The IMU cannot measure spin rate. 6000 RPM = 100 rev/s = 36,000 °/s. This far exceeds the ICM-42688's ±2000 °/s gyro range — the gyro will saturate instantly during operation. Motor speed feedback is the IR sensor's job exclusively.

What the IMU Actually Does

While the gyro saturates during spin, the accelerometer remains useful. It measures the unit's tilt relative to gravity when the display is placed at an angle. The render task reads tilt data and can rotate the displayed scene to appear upright regardless of how the unit is sitting. Secondary use: vibration anomaly detection when the display is stationary (imbalance diagnostics). Tertiary: wake/sleep gesture detection.

Enclosure Design

Base Unit

Solid 3D-printed cylinder, 60mm OD × 40mm tall. Print in PETG or ABS — not PLA, which softens at ~60°C and is unsuitable near the motor. The motor mounts vertically at center in a 24.5mm pocket (0.5mm clearance), held by friction + epoxy bead on outer circumference after alignment. Battery, base PCB, and USB-C connector ring the motor pocket. USB-C breaks out through the sidewall. The Qi TX coil mounts flush into the top face.

Rotor Shaft Adapter

A small 3D-printed hub clamps onto the motor's 2mm shaft. Design as a collet-style clamp with an M1.6 set screw pressing against a flat filed onto the motor shaft. At 6000 RPM, any shaft looseness causes catastrophic wobble. Apply threadlocker to the set screw after final alignment. The rotor PCB attaches vertically to this hub through a center hole, standing upright like a blade.

Dome (Display Guard)

Clear frosted acrylic tube, 50–55mm ID, 3mm wall, ~90mm tall. Press-fits or snap-fits into a recess on the base top. Frosting scatters LED output laterally, smoothing the POV effect. Clear acrylic gives a harsher stroboscopic look. Leave the dome top open for thermal management — the motor and LEDs generate heat and a sealed dome traps it. Source as laser-cut tube from any acrylic supplier.

Balance — Critical

At 6000 RPM, a 1g mass imbalance at 30mm radius produces centrifugal force of about 1.8N. This causes vibration, noise, bearing wear, and image jitter. The rotor PCB must be balanced around the rotation axis.

Design the PCB such that components are distributed symmetrically left-right. If the LED face is heavier than the back, add a copper pour counterweight on the opposite face. After assembly, check balance by spinning slowly by hand in a still room — any wobble is visible and must be corrected before running at full speed.

IR Sensor Placement

The TCRT5000 mounts in a small pocket in the inner top surface of the base, pointing upward at the rotor PCB underside. Position it at the rotor's radial center (directly below the shaft center) or at any consistent radial position — the reflective tab on the PCB underside passes over it once per revolution.

The reflective tab is simply white soldermask exposed on the rotor PCB underside, or a 5×5mm piece of chrome tape. Ensure the rest of the PCB underside has no other highly reflective patches that could trigger false pulses.

SPI Chain Topology

All 120 APA102C LEDs are wired in a single daisy-chain: CLK and DATA connect in series through each LED, from the ESP32-S3's SPI MOSI/CLK outputs to LED 1, then LED 1 output to LED 2, and so on through LED 120. The SPI chain terminates with no pull-up needed — APA102C handles this internally.

Arrange the chain order in the firmware to match your physical column/row layout. Row-major ordering (all 12 LEDs in row 0 first, then row 1, etc.) is the most intuitive for framebuffer indexing.

Power Routing

5V and GND should be routed as wide pours on separate layers — at 500–700mA, resistance in thin traces causes measurable voltage drop across 85mm of board length. Target ≤0.1V drop at max current. At 35µm copper, a 2mm-wide trace has about 15mΩ/cm — across 8.5cm that's ~130mΩ, producing ~90mV drop at 700mA. Widen to 3mm on 5V pour or use both layers in parallel for the power rail.

Firmware Architecture

The firmware has two completely independent domains: the base MCU firmware (STM32G031, interrupt-driven C, no RTOS) and the rotor MCU firmware (ESP32-S3, FreeRTOS, two cores). They share no code and communicate only via the IR sync signal and optionally a UART link.

Base Firmware (STM32G031)

1
Boot — init peripherals
Configure TIM1 for PWM output to motor MOSFET, TIM2 as free-running counter for IR period measurement, GPIO interrupt on IR sensor pin, I2C1 for BQ25895.
2
IR interrupt handler
On each rising edge, read TIM2 counter, compute period delta, calculate RPM, run PID update, write new duty cycle to TIM1 CCR. Reset TIM2 counter. Entire handler runs in <5µs.
3
Main loop — BQ25895 polling
Every 5 seconds, read charge status and battery voltage over I2C. Store values. Optionally transmit over UART for debugging. If battery <3.3V, reduce motor PWM cap.
4
Soft start ramp
On power-on, PID setpoint ramps from 0 to 6000 RPM over 3 seconds. Prevents current surge and mechanical shock to the rotor assembly.

Rotor Firmware (ESP32-S3)

1
Boot — Core 1 init
Initialize SPI peripheral (MOSI + CLK to APA102C chain). Configure hardware timer for column timing. Set up GPIO interrupt for IR sync pulse input. Blank all 120 LEDs.
2
IR sync ISR (Core 1)
Reset column counter to 0. Measure inter-pulse period for accurate column timing interval. Reload hardware timer. Increment revolution counter.
3
Column timer ISR (Core 1)
Fire every period/12 µs. Read framebuffer[col][0..9], serialize to APA102C format, burst over SPI. Increment col. If col == 12, col = 0. Total SPI time per column: <200µs at 1MHz SPI, far within 833µs budget.
4
Render task (Core 0, high priority)
Projects 3D scene (shapes or fluid grid) into cylindrical framebuffer. Runs at 60Hz. Takes mutex on framebuffer, writes new pixel values, releases mutex. Column ISR takes same mutex to read.
5
Fluid sim task (Core 0, normal priority)
Runs Navier-Stokes solver on 12×10 grid at 30–60Hz. Updates sim buffer. Render task reads from sim buffer each frame. Double-buffering prevents tearing.
6
IMU task (Core 0, low priority)
Reads ICM-42688 accelerometer over I2C at 10Hz. Computes tilt angle. Updates a global orientation variable that the render task uses to adjust scene rotation.

Fluid Simulation — Jos Stam Method

The fluid simulation runs on a 12×10 grid matching the cylindrical display dimensions. Each cell has a velocity field (vx, vy) and a density value. Density maps to LED color and brightness.

Algorithm Steps (per frame)

1
Add sources
Inject density and velocity at seed points. For a night-light feel, add a slow upward velocity bias and periodic density pulses at the bottom row.
2
Diffuse velocity
Spread velocity field using Gauss-Seidel iteration (2–4 iterations sufficient at 12×10 resolution). Controls fluid viscosity.
3
Project velocity
Enforce incompressibility — make the velocity field mass-conserving. Another Gauss-Seidel solve. Prevents density accumulation in corners.
4
Advect velocity + density
Move density along velocity field using bilinear interpolation (stable semi-Lagrangian advection). This is the main "flow" step.
5
Map to pixels
Each grid cell's density value is mapped through a color gradient (e.g. blue→cyan→white for a cool fluid, red→orange→yellow for a flame) and written to the framebuffer.

Python Prototype First

Before implementing on the ESP32-S3, prototype the fluid sim in Python with numpy and matplotlib. A 12×10 grid runs in milliseconds — you can tune viscosity, diffusion rate, source positions, and color mapping interactively before writing a single line of C.

# rough prototype structure import numpy as np N, M = 12, 10 # cols, rows density = np.zeros((N, M)) vx = np.zeros((N, M)) vy = np.zeros((N, M)) def step(dt, visc, diff): add_sources(density, vx, vy) diffuse(vx, visc, dt) diffuse(vy, visc, dt) project(vx, vy) advect(density, vx, vy, dt) map_to_rgb(density) # → framebuffer

Port to C once the behavior looks right in Python. The algorithm is identical — replace numpy operations with nested for-loops over your 12×10 arrays.

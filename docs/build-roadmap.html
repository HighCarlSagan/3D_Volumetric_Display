— A vertical-blade POV display in the tradition of Mitxela's Candle. 12×12 APA102C matrix on a spinning PCB, inductively powered from a stationary base, dual-IR phase lock, published end-to-end.

Concept & physics

A thin vertical PCB — the rotor blade — carries a 12×12 APA102C matrix on its front face. The blade mounts onto a motor shaft through a hole in its centerline. The motor sits in a stationary cylindrical base below. When the motor spins, the blade rotates about its own vertical centerline, and each row of LEDs sweeps a circle at its height. 144 such circles stacked produce a hollow cylindrical voxel volume that the eye perceives as a solid 3D image.

Two physical domains: the stationary base handles power, motor drive, motor speed control, and the IR emitter for rotor sync; the spinning rotor handles LEDs and rendering. They communicate only through an inductive power link (base→rotor) and two independent IR optical links (one each direction for angular alignment).

A rotating blade is geometrically efficient: 144 LEDs spun around their centerline produce the equivalent of ~21.6k voxels on a cylindrical shell. The key physical constraints — balance, inductive gap, and sync timing — all fall out of this simple mechanical picture.

Bandwidth sanity check

APA102C frame = 32 bits/LED. 144 LEDs × 32 bits = 4608 bits per column. At 8 MHz SPI that's ~576 µs per column update. At 1800 RPM = 30 Hz = 33.3 ms/rev. Budget for 120 angular slices/rev → 33.3/120 = 278 µs per slice. SPI must be ≥ 16 MHz to hit 120 slices; 8 MHz limits us to ~60 slices (6° resolution), which is still well above the perceptual threshold. Spec SPI at 20 MHz for headroom.

Power sanity check

APA102C-2020 draws ~20 mA per LED at full white. 144 × 20 mA = 2.88 A peak at 5 V = 14.4 W worst case. For realistic content (<30% average brightness, ~20% duty due to column scanning effect) the average is ~4 W. The inductive link must cover the average plus transient headroom. Firmware caps global brightness at 25% during MkI bring-up — this also protects rotor rail during inductive coupling variation.

Design choice The reference spec I worked from suggested 6000 RPM and 12×10. I pushed back to 1800 RPM and 12×12 because: (1) 1800 RPM is 11× easier on balance and bearings; (2) mitxela's candle runs at ~1800 RPM and looks great; (3) 12×12 gives square angular/vertical resolution per degree which is aesthetically nicer. 3000 RPM is achievable as a post-MkI upgrade if the mechanical build is clean.

The two independent IR paths

This is the part most people get wrong. The base has two IR components doing two different jobs, and the rotor has one doing a third:

  • BASE · OUT IR LED (pointing UP, continuous on). Provides a fixed optical beacon for the rotor. Wired straight to 5 V through a current-limiting resistor — no GPIO needed.
  • BASE · IN TCRT5000 reflective sensor (pointing UP at rotor underside). Sees a white-soldermask reflector tab on the rotor once per revolution. Output wired to STM32 GPIO with EXTI interrupt. This is the motor speed feedback.
  • ROTOR · IN Phototransistor (pointing DOWN). Sees the base's IR LED once per revolution, through a small cutout / window in the rotor PCB. Wired to ESP32-S3 GPIO with interrupt on rising edge. This is the image angular sync.

The two IR paths are completely decoupled electrically. They happen to fire at nearly the same moment each revolution (sub-ms offset) because the rotor's reflector tab and the rotor's IR window are placed at the same angular position — but nothing in the firmware depends on that. Each MCU uses its own IR pulse independently.

Why this over slip rings or a single IR Two IR paths is the cleanest way to get independent, electrically isolated sync on both sides. Slip rings at 1800 RPM wear and introduce electrical noise. Modulating data onto the inductive link is elegant but adds firmware complexity on both MCUs — not worth it for a once-per-rev signal. Two cheap optoelectronic paths is the right engineering trade.

Mechanical design & dimensions

The mechanical stack must satisfy four simultaneous constraints: (1) rigid motor mount that holds the shaft perpendicular to the base within ±0.2°, (2) coil-to-coil gap of 1–3 mm held constant across all rotation angles, (3) rotor blade balanced around its rotation axis to within a gram-mm at the worst radius, and (4) safety enclosure that fully contains the blade if a solder joint fails at speed.

Rotor hub — the critical mechanical part

The 3D-printed hub connecting motor shaft to rotor PCB is where most amateur POV builds fail. Spec:

  • Collet-style clamp: printed as a split cylinder with an M2 bolt that draws the two halves together around the motor shaft. Prints in PETG with 100% infill, 0.15 mm layer height.
  • Flat on motor shaft: file or Dremel a ~0.3 mm flat along 5 mm of the motor shaft length where the hub sits. The set screw bottoms on this flat. No flat = slip at startup torque.
  • PCB mating surface: the hub has a flat circular face that the rotor PCB bonds to with cyanoacrylate + M2 bolt through the PCB center hole into a threaded insert in the hub.
  • Height offset: hub dimensioned so that when seated on motor shaft, the rotor PCB's underside sits 10 mm above the base top face (sets TX/RX coil gap at 1.5 mm through coil thickness).
  • Threadlocker: Loctite 243 on the M2 set screw after final alignment. Non-negotiable for 1800 RPM.

Balance — every gram matters

At 1800 RPM (188.5 rad/s), a 1 g mass at 30 mm radius from the axis produces a centripetal force of 1.07 N rotating at 30 Hz. That force shows up as a sinusoidal wobble at the bearing. Cheap 683ZZ bearings tolerate this but make audible noise and wear faster. The rotor PCB must be designed for balance from day one:

  • Component layout: LEDs are the heaviest population (144 × ~30 mg = 4.3 g). Distribute evenly across the full 100 mm blade height. ESP32-S3-MINI-1 (~1 g) goes on the back face, centered on the rotation axis. Bulk cap (~1.5 g) also centered. RX coil (~3 g) centered radially, near the shaft at blade bottom.
  • Copper counterweight: the LED-loaded front face will be slightly heavier. Add a 4×80 mm copper pour on the back face opposite the LED column centerline to compensate. Calculate on final layout.
  • Trim weights: leave 3× small through-hole pads at the blade tip corners. After assembly, do static balance check by spinning slowly by hand on loose bearings; add micro-drops of solder to pads until wobble is imperceptible.
Critical Do not run the rotor outside the acrylic dome above 300 RPM. A solder joint failure at 1800 RPM launches a 2020-size LED at ~14 m/s radially. The dome is eye protection.

PCB design notes

Rotor PCB

  • Stack-up: 2-layer, 1 oz copper, FR-4, 1.6 mm standard.
  • SPI chain: 144 APA102C in a single daisy-chain, row-major order. MOSI + SCK from ESP32-S3 to LED[0] DI/CI; LED[0] DO/CO to LED[1] DI/CI; repeat. No pull-ups needed.
  • Power plane: 5 V pour on the front face underneath and around the LED strip. GND pour on back face. Vias stitch the two planes with < 5 mm spacing along the LED column. This minimizes ground loop area and supply inductance.
  • Decoupling: 100 nF 0402 every 6th LED on the 5 V rail. 10 µF bulk near the rectifier and near the ESP32. 1000 µF electrolytic near the RX coil output.
  • DRC rule: custom rule flagging any copper within 2 mm of the Ø 2.2 mm center hole. Vibration stress concentrates at the hole boundary — cracks traces there first.
  • LED orientation: all 144 LEDs oriented identically (pin 1 UP, for example), which massively simplifies SPI chain routing. Verify pin 1 marker convention against APA102C-2020 datasheet.
  • ESP32-S3 placement: centered on the rotation axis (blade center), back face. Keep the antenna cutout oriented toward one of the blade edges.
  • Phototransistor: TEFT4300 mounted on back face underneath the blade, collector facing down. The 3 mm IR window in the PCB directly above it gives optical path to base IR LED.

Base PCB

  • Stack-up: 2-layer, 1 oz copper, FR-4, 1.6 mm. Ø 78 mm disk.
  • Motor cutout: central Ø 25 mm circular cutout accommodates the motor pocket in the enclosure. All components ring around this cutout.
  • TX coil keepout: 2 mm radial keepout around the TX coil footprint. No copper traces underneath (pours ok if needed for shielding, but ideally leave clear).
  • Thermal relief on MOSFET: the AO3400 motor MOSFET dissipates 200–400 mW at 1800 RPM. Give it a 10×10 mm copper pour on drain pad.
  • USB-C: place at board edge with a cutout in the enclosure sidewall. CC1 + CC2 with 5.1 kΩ to GND (UFP config, USB 2.0 only).
  • SWD header: place at accessible edge. Samtec FTSH 2×5 0.05" pitch.
  • Star ground: separate analog ground (TCRT5000 output, STM32 analog ref) from power ground (motor, TX coil driver). Join at a single star point near the USB-C GND pin.

Firmware architecture & code structure

Two completely independent codebases. They share no code and communicate only through the IR optical pulses. Each MCU has its own repository folder, its own build system, its own test harness.

Base firmware — STM32G031

Language: C (bare-metal with STM32Cube HAL, or libopencm3 if you prefer). No RTOS. Interrupt-driven. Target binary < 16 KB.

// base/src/main.c — structure overview

// ─── Hardware map ───
// PA6  — TIM1_CH1 PWM output to motor MOSFET gate
// PA1  — EXTI1 input from TCRT5000 (rising edge = reflector tab passed)
// PB6  — USART1 TX (debug)
// PA13/PA14 — SWD

// ─── Globals ───
volatile uint32_t last_ir_tick = 0;
volatile uint32_t period_ticks = 0;    // from IR — one revolution
volatile float    rpm          = 0;
volatile float    pwm_duty     = 0.30f;  // start at 30%, ramp up

static const float RPM_SETPOINT = 1800.0f;
static const float KP           = 0.00015f;
static const float KI           = 0.00002f;
static float integral_err        = 0;

// ─── ISR: EXTI1 — fires on every IR reflector pulse ───
void EXTI0_1_IRQHandler(void) {
    if (EXTI->RPR1 & EXTI_RPR1_RPIF1) {
        uint32_t now = TIM2->CNT;
        period_ticks = now - last_ir_tick;
        last_ir_tick = now;

        rpm = 60.0f * TIM2_HZ / (float)period_ticks;

        // PI controller
        float err = RPM_SETPOINT - rpm;
        integral_err += err;
        if (integral_err > 10000) integral_err = 10000;
        if (integral_err < -10000) integral_err = -10000;

        pwm_duty += KP * err + KI * integral_err;
        if (pwm_duty > 0.95f) pwm_duty = 0.95f;
        if (pwm_duty < 0.10f) pwm_duty = 0.10f;

        TIM1->CCR1 = (uint32_t)(pwm_duty * TIM1->ARR);

        EXTI->RPR1 = EXTI_RPR1_RPIF1;  // clear flag
    }
}

int main(void) {
    hal_init();           // clocks at 64 MHz
    gpio_init();
    tim1_pwm_init(20000); // 20 kHz PWM, above audible
    tim2_counter_init();  // free-running @ 1 MHz for period measurement
    exti_init_pa1();
    uart_init(115200);

    // Soft-start: ramp PWM from 0 to 30% over 2 seconds before closing loop
    soft_start_motor();

    while (1) {
        // Main loop: log, watchdog pet, house-keeping
        if (ticks_elapsed(500)) {
            uart_printf("RPM=%.1f DUTY=%.2f\r\n", rpm, pwm_duty);
        }
        IWDG_Kick();
        __WFI();  // sleep until next interrupt
    }
}

The PI loop runs inside the IR ISR. This is intentional — the natural control rate equals the mechanical sample rate (one update per rev = 30 Hz at 1800 RPM), which is plenty fast for a motor with ~100 ms mechanical time constant. No separate PID task needed.

Rotor firmware — ESP32-S3

Language: C++ on ESP-IDF (Arduino framework also fine for MkI). Dual-core, FreeRTOS. Core 1 reserved for timing-critical ISRs and SPI; Core 0 runs WiFi + render tasks.

// rotor/main/rotor.cpp — structure overview

// ─── Hardware map ───
// GPIO11 — SPI MOSI (to APA102 DI)
// GPIO12 — SPI SCK  (to APA102 CI)
// GPIO3  — IR phototransistor input (GPIO interrupt)
// GPIO2  — onboard status LED (optional)

constexpr int COLS = 12;  // angular slices we target (derived from timing)
constexpr int ROWS = 12;  // vertical LED rows
constexpr int N_LEDS = ROWS * COLS; // 144 total LEDs, but display column count is typically higher

// Actually: the PHYSICAL matrix is 12x12 (per blade rotation we want 12 columns × 12 rows visible).
// But the ANGULAR resolution is higher — we push a new column pattern per angular slice.
// Typically COLS_ANGULAR = 60 to 120 (one SPI update per 3° to 6° of rotation).
constexpr int COLS_ANGULAR = 60;

// ─── Framebuffer: one column of 12 rows × 4 bytes (APA102 format) ───
// framebuffer[angular_slice][row] = RGB value
uint8_t framebuffer[COLS_ANGULAR][ROWS][4];  // ~2.8 KB, fits comfortably

// ─── Shared state ───
volatile uint32_t t_last_sync_us = 0;
volatile uint32_t t_period_us    = 33333;  // 1800 RPM = 33.3 ms/rev
volatile int      current_col    = 0;
SemaphoreHandle_t fb_mutex;

// ─── ISR: IR sync pulse (Core 1) ───
void IRAM_ATTR ir_sync_isr() {
    uint32_t now = esp_timer_get_time();
    t_period_us = now - t_last_sync_us;
    t_last_sync_us = now;
    current_col = 0;
    // Reload column timer for next tick
    column_timer_set_interval(t_period_us / COLS_ANGULAR);
}

// ─── ISR: column advance timer (Core 1) ───
void IRAM_ATTR column_tick_isr() {
    // Push this column's 12 LED frames over SPI
    // APA102 protocol: start frame (32b zero) + 12 × LED frame + end frame (32b 0xFF)
    spi_dma_burst(&framebuffer[current_col][0][0], ROWS * 4);

    current_col++;
    if (current_col >= COLS_ANGULAR) current_col = 0;
}

// ─── Task: render (Core 0) ───
void render_task(void* arg) {
    while (1) {
        Shape* s = active_shape;
        if (xSemaphoreTake(fb_mutex, portMAX_DELAY) == pdTRUE) {
            for (int col = 0; col < COLS_ANGULAR; col++) {
                float theta = 2.0f * M_PI * col / COLS_ANGULAR;
                for (int row = 0; row < ROWS; row++) {
                    float y = (float)row / (ROWS - 1) - 0.5f;  // -0.5 .. +0.5
                    // Shape function returns RGB given (theta, y); LED radial position r is known per row (fixed)
                    RGB c = s->sample(theta, y);
                    apa102_write(framebuffer[col][row], c.r, c.g, c.b, GLOBAL_BRIGHTNESS);
                }
            }
            xSemaphoreGive(fb_mutex);
        }
        vTaskDelay(pdMS_TO_TICKS(33));  // ~30 Hz render update
    }
}

extern "C" void app_main() {
    fb_mutex = xSemaphoreCreateMutex();

    spi_init_dma();                     // 20 MHz, half-duplex, no CS
    gpio_attach_interrupt(IR_PIN, ir_sync_isr, RISING);
    column_timer_init(column_tick_isr);

Shape system

Each shape is a function RGB sample(float theta, float y). No voxel file format, no buffers to upload. Add new shapes as C++ classes implementing the Shape interface, compile, flash. WiFi endpoint switches between them at runtime.

// rotor/main/shapes.h
struct RGB { uint8_t r, g, b; };

class Shape {
public:
    virtual RGB sample(float theta, float y) = 0;
};

class Sphere : public Shape {
    float R = 0.4f;
public:
    RGB sample(float theta, float y) override {
        float r = LED_RADIUS;  // each row is at fixed r; actually we iterate r too
        if (r*r + y*y < R*R) return { 200, 100, 40 };
        return { 0, 0, 0 };
    }
};

class Helix : public Shape {
    float pitch = 2.0f;
public:
    RGB sample(float theta, float y) override {
        float phase = fmodf(pitch * theta / (2 * M_PI) + y, 1.0f);
        if (fabs(phase) < 0.1f) return { 0, 200, 100 };
        return { 0, 0, 0 };
    }
};

// Text, waveform, Lissajous, fluid sim etc. all implemented as Shape subclasses

3-week build roadmap

Week 1 · Schematic & procurePCB lead time is the critical path
D 01
Parts order
Motor, bearings, Würth / Adafruit coil pair, 170× APA102C-2020, ESP32-S3-MINI-1, STM32G031, TCRT5000, passives. LCSC + Robu.in. 3–5 day shipping domestic.
D 02
Rotor schematic
ESP32-S3, 12×12 APA102C chain, RX coil + rectifier + LDO, phototransistor circuit, decoupling network. KiCad 8.
D 03
Base schematic
STM32G031, motor MOSFET + flyback, TX coil driver (Qi module path), TCRT5000 circuit, IR LED circuit, USB-C + buck, SWD header.
D 04–05
Rotor layout
15×100 mm blade. Component placement for balance (LEDs front, ESP32 back-center, coil near shaft). Power pour on front, ground on back. Stitch vias. DRC + ERC clean.
D 06
Base layout
Ø 78 mm disk, center motor cutout, components ringed around. Thermal relief on motor MOSFET. USB-C at edge. Fire both boards to JLCPCB same order.
D 07
Mechanical CAD
Fusion / OnShape: enclosure, top lid, motor pocket, rotor hub, dome retainer. Print while PCBs ship (5-day overlap).
Week 2 · Build & bring-upPCBs land ~D10–11 realistic from JLC to IN
D 08–10
3D print + firmware skeletons
Print enclosure + hub + lid. Write base firmware skeleton (PWM + IR EXTI + PID scaffolding). Write rotor firmware skeleton (SPI DMA + column timer). Test rotor firmware on devkit with 12 LEDs manually wired.
D 11
Rotor bring-up (static)
Solder rotor PCB. Power from bench supply via flying leads to 5 V rail (bypass inductive). Run APA102 test patterns: RGB walk, solid colors, per-column activation. Verify all 144 LEDs.
D 12
Base bring-up (static)
Solder base PCB. Program STM32G031 via SWD. Verify PWM output on scope. Verify TCRT5000 detection with a reflector held over it by hand. Inductive link bench test: measure DC output on secondary side under load (use rotor PCB as load, by hand).
D 13
Mechanical assembly
Mount motor in enclosure, press-fit bearings, install shaft, glue rotor hub, mount rotor PCB. Install TX coil in top lid. Verify coil-to-coil gap is 1.5 mm with rotor installed.
D 14
Static balance
Spin rotor by hand with power off. Observe wobble. Add trim solder to tip pads until wobble is imperceptible. Do not exceed 300 RPM without dome.
Week 3 · Integrate & shipMechanical tuning + firmware polish
D 15
RPM ramp
Dome installed. Power from inductive link only. Open-loop PWM ramp: 20% → 40% → 60% → 80%. Watch for rotor rail droop on scope probe through dome gap. Confirm TCRT5000 fires 1×/rev.
D 16
Motor PID close loop
Enable PID. Tune KP, KI until 1800 RPM setpoint holds ±1% for 5 minutes. Log over UART.
D 17
Rotor sync lock
Verify ESP32 IR interrupt fires once per revolution. Column timer fires COLS_ANGULAR × 30 Hz. Display a single stationary vertical line — should hang in space motionless.
D 18–19
Shapes
Implement Sphere, Cube, Helix, Torus. Then text ("HELLO" rendered at one angular position). Then RGB checker, Lissajous. Iterate on brightness + contrast.
D 20
WiFi upload + polish
ESPAsyncWebServer endpoint /shape. Laptop → POST JSON → active shape switches. Reduce vibration with additional trim if needed.
D 21
Ship
Long-exposure + slow-mo video. Hero photo. Write README with BOM, schematic PDFs, STL downloads, assembly guide. Push GitHub.

Honest risks

CRIT ·Balance is the #1 project killer. An unbalanced rotor at 1800 RPM damages bearings and is a safety hazard. Budget real time for iterative static balancing with trim solder. Test every intermediate speed (300 → 600 → 1000 → 1800) with the dome on.
HIGH ·Inductive power is tight. Qi 5 W coils deliver ~3.5 W at 2 mm gap in practice. Firmware must cap LED average brightness to keep rotor rail stable. Measure the rail on scope during bring-up.
HIGH ·3-week timeline assumes PCB lands on D10–11. JLCPCB to Bangalore can slip to D13. If so, compress week 3. Have a bench-wired rotor prototype ready as fallback.
MED ·Motor shaft-to-hub slip. If the set screw doesn't bite a filed flat on the shaft, the rotor slips at high torque and the IR sync drifts. File a proper flat; use threadlocker.
MED ·Rotor sync jitter. If the ESP32 IR interrupt has latency > 50 µs the image will shear visibly. Pin the ISR to Core 1 with IRAM_ATTR. Keep the ISR under 10 µs.
MED ·APA102C global brightness cap. Do not omit this. 144 LEDs × 20 mA @ full white = 2.88 A which no inductive link will supply. Cap at 25% for MkI; revisit after thermal/power measurements.
LOW ·TCRT5000 ambient light sensitivity. Direct sunlight or room IR can false-trigger. Shroud the sensor in a short opaque tube pointing up, or use a 940 nm bandpass film.

Definition of done

  • Stable 1800 ±20 RPM for 30 minutes continuous. PID locked. No bearing whine.
  • All 144 APA102C LEDs driven. Global brightness cap enforced.
  • IR sync: ESP32 column counter resets every revolution, image hangs motionless in space.
  • At least 4 shapes: sphere, helix, text, RGB pattern. Switchable over WiFi.
  • Inductive link delivers rotor rail ≥ 4.7 V under full display load.
  • Dome installed during all spin tests above 300 RPM.
  • README with hero photo, demo video (long-exposure + slow-mo), BOM, gerbers, STLs, firmware binaries, build guide.
